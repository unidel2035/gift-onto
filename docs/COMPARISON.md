# gift vs. every neighbouring ontology

A gift ontology is unusual enough that the honest question is not "is it better?" but "is it *different*, and was the difference necessary?" This document compares `gift-onto` against every ontology family it borders, on a fixed set of axes, and ends with the formal reduction that places it underneath them.

## The axes

For each ontology we ask:

1. **Atom** — what is the primitive?
2. **Reversible?** — can a recorded fact be undone/edited/rolled back?
3. **Symmetric?** — is giving the same as receiving; does it net to zero?
4. **Value?** — does the data itself carry worth/normativity, or is value external?
5. **Person?** — is the subject irreducible, or an endpoint/attribute?
6. **Conserves?** — must the total be preserved, or can there be surplus?
7. **Telos** — what is the ontology *for*?

---

## 1. Object / state ontologies — RDF, OWL, schema.org, property graphs

- **Atom:** a thing and its properties; the triple `(subject, predicate, object)`.
- Reversible: n/a — they model state, which is mutated freely.
- Symmetric / Value / Person: not applicable / no / no (the subject is an identifier with attributes).
- Conserves: n/a. Telos: *describe what is.*

**Difference from gift:** these have no concept of an *act* at all, let alone an irreversible or valued one. They answer "what is there"; they are mute about "what happened" and "what was given." gift-onto is orthogonal, not competing — you could express a W snapshot in RDF, but RDF cannot supply the axioms.

---

## 2. Foundational / upper ontologies — BFO, DOLCE, SUMO

- **Atom:** categories of being — continuant (endures) vs. occurrent (happens).
- They are *meta*: frameworks other ontologies specialise.
- Value: no — deliberately value-free; that is their discipline.
- Telos: *give every domain ontology a shared backbone of being.*

**Difference from gift:** BFO would classify a GiftAct as an occurrent and a Person as an independent continuant — and stop there. It cannot say the act is irreversible, asymmetric, or good; those are not categories of *being*, they are properties of *giving*. gift-onto is a domain ontology that could sit on a BFO backbone but adds exactly what BFO refuses to: normativity.

---

## 3. Provenance — W3C PROV-O, CIDOC-CRM

- **Atom:** `entity / activity / agent`; "this entity was generated by that activity, associated with that agent."
- Reversible: descriptive — PROV records what happened, append-only in practice, but makes no irreversibility *commitment* and no rollback *prohibition*.
- Symmetric: no (derivation is directed) — **this is PROV's closest kinship to gift.**
- Value: **no** — PROV is purposely neutral; it tells you *who did what to what*, never whether it was owed or free.
- Person: agent-as-endpoint, described, not irreducible.
- Telos: *trace derivation and attribution.*

**Difference from gift:** PROV-O is the nearest *descriptive* relative. Both are directed graphs of agents and acts. The gap is normativity and irreversibility-as-axiom: PROV says "B was derived from A by agent X"; gift says "X **gave** B to Y, at weight w, and cannot take it back, and Y is now bound." PROV describes; gift obligates.

---

## 4. Event ontologies / event sourcing — boldsea, CQRS, EDA

- **Atom:** the event — "what happened, with what, recorded by whom."
- Reversible: **append-only** (this is shared with gift — boldsea's "data is never overwritten or deleted, only added" is, word for word, gift's irreversibility).
- Symmetric: the graph is directed *causally* (event → predecessor event), but the edge is a causal reference, not a signed value between persons.
- Value: **no** — the event is neutral. It records that something happened, not whether it was good, owed, or free.
- Person: present as "who recorded," but the subject is not the bearer of accumulating moral weight.
- Conserves: n/a. Telos: *reconstruct state and causation over time.*

**Difference from gift (the boldsea case specifically):** this is the most important comparison, because boldsea proves the *skeleton* — append-only causal temporal graph + federation + AI agent on top — is sound and sells. gift-onto shares that skeleton exactly. The single difference: **boldsea's edge is a causal reference between events; gift's edge is a signed, weighted act between persons.** Both graphs are directed; only gift's direction is *morally loaded*. An AI agent over boldsea reasons about *causation*; an AI agent over gift reasons about *trust and reciprocity*. Drop the weight and the person, and gift becomes an event ontology.

---

## 5. Economic / accounting ontologies — REA (McCarthy)

- **Atom:** `resource / event / agent`, with the **duality axiom** — every economic event has a paired counter-event (give ↔ take), and they must balance.
- Reversible: economically, yes — refunds, reversals, corrections net the books.
- Symmetric: **structurally dual** — this is REA's defining commitment.
- Value: **exchange-value** — worth is the market/price of the resource, external to the relationship.
- Person: agents as endpoints of resource flows.
- Conserves: **yes** — the model exists to make things net to zero (that is what accounting *is*).
- Telos: *represent balanced exchange so the books reconcile.* (Influence: ebXML, ISO 15944-4.)

**Difference from gift:** REA is the formalisation of exactly what the gift is *not*. Its duality axiom *requires* the counter-event that the gift refuses; its conservation forbids the surplus that the gift produces; its value is price, not moral weight. REA models the commodity half of Mauss's distinction; gift models the gift half.

---

## 6. Enterprise / transaction ontologies — DEMO (Jan Dietz)

- **Atom:** the **transaction pattern** — `request → promise → execute → state → accept` between two roles (initiator, executor), built from **C-acts** (coordination/commitment) and **P-acts** (production).
- Reversible: a transaction can be declined, revoked, rejected within the pattern.
- Symmetric: **bilateral** — the transaction *completes only on mutual `accept`.*
- Value: commitment/obligation (rich on this axis — DEMO is genuinely about who owes whom what) but not a numeric accumulating weight.
- Person: actor-roles, well modelled, but again endpoints of a pattern.
- Telos: *capture the essence of an organisation as commitments between actors.*

**Difference from gift:** DEMO is the most sophisticated transaction ontology and the most instructive contrast. Its core — *completion requires bilateral accept* — is precisely the axiom gift inverts: the gift **completes at giving**, and acceptance is the recipient's *freedom*, not a completion condition. DEMO models obligation-as-agreement; gift models obligation-as-irreversible-fact. In DEMO you are bound when you both say yes; in gift you are bound the moment the gift is given, whatever you say.

---

## 7. Blockchain / smart-contract ontologies

- **Atom:** the transaction on an immutable ledger.
- Reversible: **no — irreversible** (this is shared with gift, and *only* this).
- Symmetric: a **symmetric transfer** of fungible value; A−x, B+x.
- Value: price / token amount.
- Person: deliberately **removed** — addresses, not persons; pseudonymity is a feature.
- Telos: ***transact without trust.***

**Difference from gift — the sharpest in this document.** Blockchain and gift are the two irreversible ledgers, and their purposes are exact opposites:

> Blockchain makes value transfer possible **without** trust — it engineers the person *out*, so that no relationship is needed. gift-onto makes trust **accumulate** — it puts the person *in*, so that relationship is the whole point.

One is trustless by design; the other is trust-building by design. They share the append-only immutable substrate and nothing else. If you remember one sentence from this file, remember this pair.

---

## 8. Financial domain ontologies — FIBO

- **Atom:** financial instruments, contracts, parties (a large specialised vocabulary on a foundational base).
- Value: market value; Person: legal entities; Conserves: yes (it is finance).
- Telos: *standardise the description of financial instruments and contracts.*

**Difference from gift:** FIBO is a rich domain vocabulary for the world of priced, reversible, conserved contracts. It is REA's commitments at industrial scale. It has no room for, and no need for, an irreversible asymmetric gift with surplus — that is simply not what finance is.

---

## 9. The anthropological ancestor — Mauss, *The Gift*

Not a computational ontology, but the conceptual root. Marcel Mauss (1925) distinguished the **gift economy** (relationship-creating, obligation-bearing, status-laden) from **commodity exchange** (impersonal, balanced, closed). Every ontology in §5–§8 formalises the *commodity* half. None formalises the *gift* half.

> **gift-onto is the attempt to give the gift half of Mauss its own computational ontology** — directed by a theological reading of the gift (kenosis, theosis, anamnesis) rather than a sociological one, but addressing the gap Mauss named a century ago.

## The reduction

The table is not just a list — it is a hierarchy. Read the differences as subtractions and the neighbours fall out of the gift:

```
gift act
  − the person, − the weight, − the direction         → event           (boldsea, CQRS)
  + a mandatory counter-act, + rollback, + conservation → transaction    (REA, DEMO, ACID)
  collapse to the result, drop the act                → object / state  (RDF, schema.org)
  keep irreversibility, remove the person             → blockchain transfer
```

The claim is not supremacy but **priority**: the gift is the more general act, and exchange is the special case where a counter-movement was demanded and a rollback permitted. This is falsifiable — show an existing ontology that already carries *signed, asymmetric, irreversible value between irreducible persons, with surplus*, and the priority claim fails. To our knowledge, none does. That gap is why `gift-onto` was built — see [WHY.md](WHY.md).
